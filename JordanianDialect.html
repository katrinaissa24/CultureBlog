A Peek Into Jordanian Dialect

Exploring the different dialects of Jordanian Arabic across districts.

Jordan, a beautiful country in the heart of the Middle East, is known for its rich history, breathtaking landscapes, and warm hospitality. One of the most lovely things about Jordanian culture is our dialect—a unique blend of Levantine Arabic with its own charm and expression.

The Jordanian dialect is known to being one of the closest to fusha Arabic, or Modern Standard Arabic (MSA).

The Linguistic Diversity in Different Parts of Jordan

Within different governorates in Jordan lies different Jordanian dialects, which may indicate sometimes where a person is coming from. In this post, I will be introducing you to the three main ones.

“Everything in this world can be robbed and stolen, except one thing; this one thing is the love that emanates from a human being towards a solid commitment to a conviction or cause.” ― Ghassan Kanafani

Ghassan Kanafani’s words remind us that while much can be taken from a people, the love and commitment they hold for their identity and culture remain untouchable. In Jordan, dialects are more than just ways of speaking—they are deeply rooted expressions of history, belonging, and pride. The preservation and use of these dialects reflect a collective commitment to cultural heritage, a form of love that resists erasure even in the face of globalization and social change.

Central Dialect

The Ammani dialect, representing the urban form of South-Levantine Arabic, features unique phonetic shifts that distiguishes it from other regional dialects.

Examples:

  • The letter "ق / k" is often pronounced as "g" or "a / أ", as in gahwe or ahwe (coffee) instead of qahwa.
  • The letter "ذ" becomes "d / د", as in dou2 (to taste) instead of thoug
  • The letter "ث / th" becomes "ت / t", as in kteer (a lot) instead of ktheer

Northern Dialect

In northern Jordan, including cities like Irbid, Jerash, Ajloun, and Mafraq, the dialect starts to become heavier.

Examples:

  • The letter "ك / k" is often pronounced as "ch", for example keefak (how are you) becomes chefak.
  • The letter "ق / k" is often pronounced as "g", for example kareb (come closer) becomes gareb.
  • The pronounciation for letters "ظ" "ث" "ذ" remains the same. for example:
    • "ثلاثه/ thalatha" (three) would remain the same, while it may be pronounced as "talata" in districts like Amman.
    • "ظروف / thorouf" (circumstances) which remains the same, while it may be pronounced as "zorouf" in districts like Amman.

Southern Dialect

The southern region of Jordan, including cities like Karak, Tafilah and Ma'an, reflects a dialect influenced heavily by Bedouin traditions due to its geographic proximity to Bedouin areas. Here, the dialect often adopts to more ancient, rugged forms of Arabic.

  • Words like "بدي / bedi" (I want) would be pronounced as "ودي / waddi"
  • Words like "لويش / lawesh" (why), which is more commmonly spoken in the North, or "ليش / lesh" which is more commonly spoken in Amman, would be pronounced as "لويه / laweh".
  • The letter "ك / k" has a deeper emphasis in words, being pronounced as kh instead of k, as in "khefak" instead of "kefak" or "keefak which is more commonly spoken in Amman
  • The letter "ت / t" can be pronounced as "ث / th", as in "ktheir" (a lot) instead of "kteer"

Unity in Diversity

The variety of dialects spoken across Jordan is a testament to the country's unity in diversity. While each voice is distinct, we all contribute to a common national identity, with each area having something unique to share with others.

For comparison, in Amman we would say "Wislou" (arrived) whereas in Salt they would say "Weslen".

More Than Words

The Jordanian dialect is a beautiful reflection of the country’s linguistic diversity and cultural richness. From the heartfelt expressions of the Salt region to the urban rhythm of Amman, the melodic tones of the North, and the Bedouin-rooted strength of the South, each dialect carries a story of its people and place.
