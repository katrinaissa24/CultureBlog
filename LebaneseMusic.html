Music April 7, 2024 By Adam Chebli, Hussein Najem, Jad Moussa

The Rhythm of Lebanon: How Music Keeps Our Culture Alive

Lebanese Caracalla Dance Theatre performing vibrant traditional folk dances.

When people ask me what Lebanese culture sounds like, I don't give them a history lesson—I play them a song. Because in Lebanon, music isn't just art; it's how we laugh, how we mourn, how we remember, and how we resist.

A Soundtrack for Every Situation

You can hear it everywhere in Beirut, whether it's the lively beat of dabke at a village wedding, Nancy Ajram's pop hits playing in taxis, or Fairuz's voice drifting from a bakery at dawn. These sounds are associated with some of my earliest memories, such as my cousins teaching me dabke steps at family gatherings and my grandmother humming "Ya Tayr" while cooking. The traditional zajal poetry competitions have changed into thrilling musical competitions, and our celebrations even have their own beats.

Not Only Notes

Lebanese music is unique because it reflects our identity by blending Arab legacy, Mediterranean warmth, and contemporary invention. Sung in our melodic dialect, the lyrics tell tales of love ("Habibi" isn't only a word; it's a thousand songs), desire for the mountains, and the small pleasures of everyday life. The heartbeat of the tabla, the passionate cries of the oud, and the sparkling tones of the qanun all speak for themselves.

An Overseas Bridge

Music is a lifeline for Lebanese living overseas. Every Sunday in Australia, my aunt plays Fayrouz "to smell the jasmine of home again." One iconic dabke song can transform strangers into family at international gatherings. Our music's strength lies in the fact that it not only reflects culture but also maintains it no matter where we are.
